# The Beggar

**Type:** Minor NPC — unnamed, recurring
**Campaign Appearances:** CAMPAIGN 5 — Loss, Legacy, and Lament

---

## Overview

An outwardly mundane human man, mid-50s to early 60s, a known fixture around Veranath who's never previously seemed supernatural. Present in the Cat's Pajamas the moment Teyou Zhiang dies and reacts with a throwaway "Huh. He's never died before" before delivering an unprompted, formal seven-worlds prophecy when Magerna touches him — then walking out as if nothing happened.

Bas's insight check on him in Session 0.5 (a 28) confirmed he wasn't hiding anything on a surface level, but couldn't get a full read — enough of an open question to send Bas and Magerna after him on foot the following session.

---

## Session 1 Session Detail

Tailed through Veranath's streets at dusk, apparently oblivious to being followed. Attacked without warning by a goblin — later revealed to be a young teenager, animated/controlled by a cursed, self-healing shard of broken Tychonium (see [The Shard](../Items/The Shard)) — and stabbed repeatedly before Bas ends the fight by severing the goblin's hand. Magerna's Death Ward keeps him alive through the attack; Cure Wounds fully heals his body afterward.

Once lucid, reveals under questioning that he's been reliving the same day on a loop for an unknown span of time — waking, begging, watching Teyou drink at the same table each night, and starting over. Today is the first time, in that loop, that Teyou has actually died. Magerna's Break Enchantment finds nothing to break; he was never charmed, possessed, or frightened — the damage is to his mind, not from any spell effect the party can identify, and it doesn't lift. Magerna confirms he's "more or less innocent... a genuinely good person who made some poor decisions and ended up in a bad position because of it."

Gets up and walks off alone, headed north toward the Hall of the Owls, after Magerna's farewell: *"I will remember you and I will mourn you."* His fate and whereabouts after this point are unknown.
